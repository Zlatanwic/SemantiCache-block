\title{SieveKV: Semantic Multi-Signal Eviction for Long-Context Retrieval}

\begin{abstract}
The key-value (KV) cache is the dominant memory bottleneck during long-context
LLM inference.
Existing eviction policies select tokens to retain based on recency, cumulative
attention, or query-agnostic importance---signals that degrade sharply when the
answer depends on a small factual span buried in a long prompt.
We present \textbf{SieveKV}, a serving-time KV cache eviction policy that
combines five lightweight signals---attention mass, information density, head
entropy, query relevance, and factual-token likelihood---with role-aware pinning
and contiguous block retention.
On the RULER Needle-in-a-Haystack benchmark with \texttt{Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct},
SieveKV achieves 70\% retrieval accuracy at a 20\% cache budget, surpassing
even the full-KV baseline (58\%) and outperforming the best baseline, SnapKV
(30\%), by 40 percentage points.
At the most aggressive 10\% budget, SieveKV reaches 80\%---while all
baselines fall below 40\%.
A cross-architecture evaluation on \texttt{Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct} confirms that
the method transfers effectively without recalibration, achieving 84\% accuracy
within 6~points of the full-KV baseline.
A multi-needle variant requiring simultaneous recall of four scattered facts
shows SieveKV reaching 92.5\%---surpassing even full KV (80\%)---while
the best baseline (SnapKV) plateaus at 70\%.
An ablation study shows that the semantic signals act synergistically: removing
any single component degrades accuracy by at most 4\%, but stripping all
semantic signals reduces accuracy from 70\% to 8\%.
Systems profiling demonstrates that SieveKV's per-step eviction overhead
(8.86\,ms) accounts for under 4\% of total decode time---a modest cost for a
40-point accuracy gain over the strongest baseline.
These results indicate that query-aware, multi-signal token scoring is a
practical and effective strategy for memory-efficient long-context inference
in retrieval-heavy workloads.
\keywords{KV cache eviction \and Long-context inference \and Query-aware systems \and Memory-efficient serving}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ================================================================

Large language models (LLMs) increasingly operate over long input contexts,
processing thousands to millions of tokens during a single inference request.
The key-value (KV) cache---which stores intermediate attention states to avoid
redundant computation during autoregressive decoding---has emerged as the
first-order memory bottleneck in this regime.
A single 3B-parameter model serving a 4K-token prompt allocates hundreds of
megabytes to cached key and value states, and the cost grows linearly with
sequence length.
When model weights already saturate accelerator memory, the cache forces a
direct trade-off between context length, batch size, and hardware cost.

A growing body of work addresses this bottleneck through \emph{token-level
eviction}: selectively discarding cached states for tokens deemed unimportant,
thereby reducing the memory footprint of the KV cache while preserving
generation quality.
StreamingLLM~\cite{xiao2023streamingllm} retains attention sinks and a sliding
window of recent tokens.
H2O~\cite{zhang2023h2o} identifies heavy-hitter tokens by cumulative attention
scores.
SnapKV~\cite{li2024snapkv} prunes at prefill time using observation-window
attention.
KVzip~\cite{he2025kvzip} scores tokens via a query-agnostic context-reconstruction
task.
These methods reduce memory effectively, but they share a common failure mode:
when the answer depends on a small factual span embedded among thousands of
distractor tokens, generic importance signals cannot reliably distinguish
\emph{answer-critical} tokens from \emph{merely frequent} ones.

This limitation is particularly acute in \emph{retrieval-intensive} workloads
---question answering, multi-hop reasoning, RAG---where the model must locate
a specific factual span among thousands of distractors~\cite{hsieh2024ruler,modarressi2025nolima}.
We observe that retrieval-oriented prompts contain a structured query whose
intent can guide eviction: a query-aware policy can exploit the question's
vocabulary to boost retention of query-aligned, content-rich tokens---precisely
those most likely to carry the answer.

This paper presents \textbf{SieveKV}, a serving-time KV cache eviction
policy designed for retrieval-intensive long-context workloads.
SieveKV makes three contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Multi-signal scoring.}
  A composite retention score that combines five lightweight, complementary
  signals---cumulative attention mass, information density, head entropy,
  query relevance, and factual-token likelihood---to identify answer-critical
  tokens under tight memory budgets (\S\ref{sec:scoring}).

\item \textbf{Structured retention.}
  A block-level selection mechanism with role-aware pinning that preserves
  local positional continuity and protects task-critical spans (system
  instructions, user query) from eviction (\S\ref{sec:pinning}).

\item \textbf{Comprehensive evaluation.}
  A systematic evaluation on the RULER Needle-in-a-Haystack
  benchmark~\cite{hsieh2024ruler}---including a multi-needle variant---against
  five baselines spanning 2023--2025, across two model architectures (Qwen and
  Llama), showing that SieveKV is the only policy whose accuracy
  \emph{increases} as the cache budget shrinks and as the number of
  simultaneously required facts grows (\S\ref{sec:results}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%% ================================================================

We organize prior work along three dimensions and position SieveKV.

\paragraph{Eviction timing.}
\textbf{Progressive} methods evict during decoding:
StreamingLLM~\cite{xiao2023streamingllm} retains attention sinks and a
recency window; H2O~\cite{zhang2023h2o} keeps heavy-hitter tokens by
cumulative attention.
\textbf{One-shot} methods prune once at prefill:
SnapKV~\cite{li2024snapkv} scores by observation-window attention;
KVzip~\cite{he2025kvzip} by context-reconstruction contribution.
SieveKV follows the progressive paradigm.

\paragraph{Scoring signal.}
Recency (StreamingLLM), attention (H2O, SnapKV), and reconstruction
(KVzip) signals are all \emph{query-agnostic}---they cannot distinguish
answer-bearing tokens from structurally similar distractors.
SieveKV combines attention with query-aware and content-aware signals.

\paragraph{Selection granularity.}
Most methods select individual tokens, risking fragmented retention.
SieveKV operates at the \emph{contiguous block} level, preserving
local positional continuity.

\subsection{Orthogonal approaches}

KV cache quantization~\cite{hooper2024kvquant,liu2024kivi} reduces bits
per token rather than token count, and is orthogonal to eviction---combining
$b{=}20\%$ eviction with 2-bit quantization would yield ${\sim}20{\times}$
memory reduction.
Grouped-query attention (GQA)~\cite{ainslie2023gqa} reduces KV heads;
both models in our evaluation already use GQA, so SieveKV provides
additional savings on top.
Learned eviction approaches train auxiliary predictors but require
per-model fine-tuning; SieveKV instead uses architecture-independent
heuristics that transfer without retraining.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}
%% ================================================================

SieveKV is a progressive KV cache eviction policy that operates during
autoregressive decoding.
At each decode step, it assigns a composite retention score to every cached
token and selects a budget-sized subset of tokens to retain.
The policy comprises three components: multi-signal token scoring
(\S\ref{sec:scoring}), role-aware pinning (\S\ref{sec:pinning}), and
contiguous block retention (\S\ref{sec:blocks}).

\subsection{Overview}

Figure~\ref{fig:framework} illustrates the two-phase pipeline.
During the \textbf{prefill phase}, the full prompt is processed through the
model's forward pass, producing the initial KV cache and attention weights.
Simultaneously, a \emph{semantic analyzer} computes static signals for all
prompt tokens: role tags, information density, query relevance, and
factual-token likelihood.
These signals are computed once and cached for reuse during decoding.

During the \textbf{decode phase}, each generated token triggers the following
pipeline:
(1)~the model produces the next token and attention weights;
(2)~the attention tracker accumulates attention statistics;
(3)~a budget check determines whether eviction is needed;
(4)~if the cache exceeds the budget, multi-signal scores are computed,
blocks are ranked, and low-scoring tokens are evicted.

\subsection{Multi-signal token scoring}
\label{sec:scoring}

SieveKV assigns each cached token a composite retention score by
combining five signals.
The first three are \emph{attention-derived} signals computed from the
model's own attention weights; the remaining two are \emph{content-derived}
signals computed from token-level features.

\paragraph{Attention mass ($s_\alpha$).}
The cumulative attention received by each token across all layers $l$ and
KV heads $h$ during prefill and decoding:
\begin{equation}
  s_\alpha(i) = \sum_{l=1}^{L} \sum_{h=1}^{H} \sum_{t} A_{l,h}(t, i)
\end{equation}
where $A_{l,h}(t, i)$ is the attention weight from query position $t$ to
key position $i$ at layer $l$, head $h$.
Tokens with high cumulative attention are structurally important to the
model's computation.

\paragraph{Head entropy ($s_\gamma$).}
The entropy of the attention distribution across KV heads for each token,
capturing tokens that are broadly attended to across multiple heads rather
than dominated by a single head:
\begin{equation}
  s_\gamma(i) = -\sum_{h=1}^{H} p_h(i) \log p_h(i), \quad
  p_h(i) = \frac{\bar{A}_h(i)}{\sum_{h'} \bar{A}_{h'}(i)}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{A}_h(i)$ is the mean attention to token $i$ from head $h$
across all query positions.

\paragraph{Information density ($s_\beta$).}
A local content-richness score estimated from character-level statistics
within a sliding window of $w{=}32$ tokens.
The score combines four features: digit ratio (+0.3), code-symbol
ratio (+0.2), lexical diversity (+0.3), and punctuation ratio ($-$0.2).
This signal favors content-rich tokens (numbers, identifiers, technical
terms) over function words and formatting tokens.

\paragraph{Query relevance ($s_q$).}
Character $n$-gram overlap between each token span and the latest user
query, computed via token-ID set intersection within a sliding window.
This signal biases retention toward tokens that share vocabulary with the
downstream question, enabling the policy to focus on query-aligned content.

\paragraph{Factual-token likelihood ($s_f$).}
Pattern-based detection of token classes most likely to carry
answer-critical information: digits (+0.25), uppercase tokens (+0.12),
month names (+0.28), measurement units (+0.28), and entity-like tokens
such as proper names and acronyms (+0.45).
This signal provides a content-aware prior that complements the
query-specific signal.

\paragraph{Score composition.}
The attention-derived signals are mixed into a base score:
\begin{equation}
  s_{\text{base}}(i) = \alpha \cdot \hat{s}_\alpha(i) + \beta \cdot \hat{s}_\beta(i) + \gamma \cdot \hat{s}_\gamma(i)
\end{equation}
where $\hat{s}$ denotes min-max normalization and $\alpha{=}0.4$,
$\beta{=}0.3$, $\gamma{=}0.3$.
Query relevance and factual likelihood are added as bonuses with minimum
weight floors ($w_q{\geq}0.30$, $w_f{\geq}0.40$) to ensure they remain
effective under normalization:
\begin{equation}
  s(i) = s_{\text{base}}(i) + w_q \cdot \hat{s}_q(i) + w_f \cdot \hat{s}_f(i)
\end{equation}

The weight floors prevent the query-aware and factual signals from being
dominated by the attention signals, which tend to have larger dynamic range.
This design choice reflects the insight that for retrieval tasks, a token's
relevance to the query is at least as important as its generic structural
importance.

The bonus floors are set higher than base weights because the bonus signals
have systematically lower magnitude (query relevance peaks at ${\sim}0.3$
vs.\ ${\sim}1.0$ for normalized attention); the factual floor exceeds
the query floor because factual patterns are sparser (${\sim}$2\% of
haystack tokens).
The ablation (\S\ref{sec:ablation}) confirms robustness: removing any
single signal costs at most 4\% accuracy.

\subsection{Role-aware pinning}
\label{sec:pinning}

Before score-based selection, SieveKV assigns \emph{infinite retention
priority} to three classes of tokens, ensuring they are never evicted
regardless of their computed scores:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{System-prompt tokens.}
  All tokens from the system instruction are pinned, preserving the task
  specification that governs the model's behavior.

\item \textbf{User-query tail.}
  The trailing $k{=}16$ tokens of the latest user message are pinned,
  preserving the specific question being asked.
  Pinning only the tail (rather than the entire user message) avoids
  consuming excessive budget when the user message includes a long context
  passage.

\item \textbf{Recent decode window.}
  A sliding window of the $r{=}12$ most recently generated tokens is
  pinned, preserving autoregressive coherence and preventing the model
  from losing track of its own recent output.
\end{itemize}

These role-aware rules exploit the structured roles in chat-formatted
prompts to make retention decisions that purely attention-based policies
cannot.

\subsection{Contiguous block retention}
\label{sec:blocks}

Token-level eviction can fragment positional continuity, causing degraded
outputs.
SieveKV groups unpinned tokens into contiguous blocks of $B{=}16$
and ranks blocks by their \emph{safest member}:
\begin{equation}
  S_{\text{block}}(b) = \max_{i \in b} s(i)
\end{equation}

This max-based scoring ensures that a single critical token (e.g., a
needle value embedded among distractor text) protects its surrounding
context from eviction.
Blocks are selected greedily in descending score order until the budget
is filled.
When the remaining budget is smaller than a full block, the policy
retains the highest-scoring tokens within the partial block and restores
their original positional order.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:eviction} summarizes the complete eviction procedure.
Note that in our scoring convention, \emph{higher} scores correspond to higher retention priority; pinned tokens receive $+\infty$ to guarantee they are always retained.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{SieveKV eviction at each decode step}
\label{alg:eviction}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE KV cache $\mathcal{C}$, budget $B$, attention tracker $\mathcal{T}$
\ENSURE Pruned cache $\mathcal{C}'$ with $|\mathcal{C}'| \leq B$
\IF{$|\mathcal{C}| \leq B$}
  \RETURN $\mathcal{C}$ \COMMENT{No eviction needed}
\ENDIF
\STATE $\mathcal{P} \leftarrow$ \textsc{PinnedMask}(system $\cup$ query-tail $\cup$ recent)
\STATE $s \leftarrow$ \textsc{MultiSignalScore}($\mathcal{C}$, $\mathcal{T}$)
\STATE $s[i] \leftarrow +\infty$ for all $i \in \mathcal{P}$ \COMMENT{Pin tokens (always retained)}
\STATE Partition unpinned tokens into blocks of size 16
\STATE Score each block $b$: $S_b \leftarrow \max_{i \in b} s(i)$
\STATE Select top-$k$ blocks to fill remaining budget
\STATE $\mathcal{C}' \leftarrow \mathcal{P} \cup$ selected block tokens
\RETURN $\mathcal{C}'$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Worked example}
\label{sec:walkthrough}

Consider a RULER NIAH prompt with ${\sim}$3700 tokens at $b{=}20\%$
(budget: 742 tokens), containing a needle
\texttt{``One of the special magic numbers for Kate is: 24592.''}
at 50\% depth.
After pinning system/query/decode tokens (${\sim}$48 tokens), the
remaining ${\sim}$3650 tokens are grouped into 16-token blocks.
The needle's block scores highest because its tokens trigger elevated
factual likelihood ($s_f{=}0.25$ vs.\ $<$0.05 for essay tokens),
information density ($s_\beta{\approx}0.7$ vs.\ ${\sim}0.3$), and
query relevance ($s_q{\approx}0.7$ vs.\ ${\sim}0$).
The 185 lowest-scoring blocks are evicted, yet the needle is preserved---
illustrating why multi-signal scoring outperforms single-signal approaches.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}
%% ================================================================

\subsection{Models}

We evaluate on two model architectures to assess cross-architecture
generality:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct}~\cite{qwen2025qwen25}: 36 layers,
  16 attention heads, 2 KV heads (GQA), head dimension 128, ChatML
  chat template.
  This is the primary evaluation model.

\item \textbf{Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct}~\cite{meta2024llama32}:
  28 layers, 24 attention heads, 8 KV heads (GQA), head dimension 128,
  Llama-style chat template.
  This serves as a cross-architecture validation.
\end{itemize}

Both models are served with 4-bit NF4 quantization via BitsAndBytes on a
single NVIDIA GPU with 48\,GB VRAM.
SieveKV's signal weights are fixed across both architectures---no
per-model calibration is performed.

\subsection{Benchmark}

We evaluate SieveKV on the RULER Needle-in-a-Haystack benchmark,
a controlled retrieval task designed to isolate eviction policy quality.

\textbf{RULER Needle-in-a-Haystack (NIAH)}~\cite{hsieh2024ruler}:
Randomized key-value needles (e.g.,
``One of the special magic numbers for Kate is: 24592'') are inserted at
controlled depths into Paul Graham essay haystacks of approximately 1800
target tokens.
After chat template expansion, prompts contain ${\sim}$3700 tokens (Qwen)
or ${\sim}$3400 tokens (Llama).
We use an exact substring-match criterion (string\_match\_all in our
implementation): a run is counted as correct only if all needle values
appear as substrings in the model output.
The NIAH task is specifically designed to stress-test a model's ability to
locate and retrieve a specific factual span from a long context---exactly
the scenario where query-agnostic eviction policies fail.

\subsection{Baselines}

We compare against five eviction policies spanning three years of research:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Full KV}: No eviction; retains all tokens.
  Serves as the accuracy reference and the latency/memory reference.

\item \textbf{StreamingLLM}~\cite{xiao2023streamingllm} (ICLR 2024):
  Retains 4 attention-sink tokens and a sliding window of recent tokens.
  Progressive eviction during decoding.

\item \textbf{H2O}~\cite{zhang2023h2o} (NeurIPS 2023):
  Retains heavy-hitter tokens ranked by cumulative attention.
  Progressive eviction during decoding.

\item \textbf{SnapKV}~\cite{li2024snapkv} (NeurIPS 2024):
  One-shot pruning at prefill using observation-window attention from the
  final prompt tokens.

\item \textbf{KVzip}~\cite{he2025kvzip} (NeurIPS 2025):
  One-shot pruning using reconstruction-based scoring from a dedicated
  context-reconstruction forward pass.
\end{enumerate}

All policies operate under a \emph{shared-index constraint}: the same
token indices are retained across all layers and KV heads.
This constraint simplifies implementation and enables fair comparison,
though it disadvantages methods originally designed for per-head selection
(SnapKV, KVzip).

\subsection{Evaluation protocol}

We conduct six evaluation campaigns totaling ${\sim}$2900 runs:
main accuracy (900 runs, 6 policies $\times$ 3 budgets $\times$ 50 trials),
budget sensitivity (1000 runs, 8-point grid),
multi-needle retrieval (150 Qwen + 90 Llama runs, $k{\in}\{1,2,4\}$),
ablation (350 runs, 7 variants),
context scaling (250 runs, 1800--32\,000\,t),
and cross-architecture validation (150 Llama runs).
Systems overhead profiling measures latency and memory over 3 runs.

Generation uses greedy decoding (temperature\,=\,0, top-p\,=\,1.0) with
max\_new\_tokens\,=\,32, matching the exact-match evaluation criterion.
Cache budget $b$ refers to the fraction of \texttt{KV\_entries} retained per
layer and is applied uniformly across all layers and KV heads.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%% ================================================================

\paragraph{Distractor filtering.}
SieveKV's counter-intuitive improvement under tighter budgets
arises from a \emph{distractor filtering} effect: aggressive eviction
removes tokens that are structurally similar to the needle but
irrelevant to the query, concentrating the model's attention on
answer-critical spans.
The full-KV baseline retains all ${\sim}$3700 tokens---including
thousands of semantic distractors---and achieves only 58\%.
Figure~\ref{fig:signals} confirms this mechanism: query relevance
provides $3.5{\times}$ discrimination between needle and haystack
tokens, while factual likelihood adds $1.6{\times}$.
The combination reliably ranks the needle block above all 228
haystack blocks.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{signal_distribution.pdf}
\caption{Signal score distributions for needle (30 tokens) vs.\ haystack
(3653 tokens) at 50\% depth.
Query relevance provides the strongest discrimination ($3.5{\times}$
ratio); the combination of all three signals reliably distinguishes
the needle block from all haystack blocks.}
\label{fig:signals}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Overhead--accuracy trade-off.}
SieveKV's per-step eviction cost (8.86\,ms) reduces throughput
to 3.4\,tok/s---roughly half the full-KV baseline.
This throughput gap is a limitation for latency-sensitive serving;
three optimization paths can close it:
(1)~incremental scoring (content signals are static after prefill),
(2)~amortized eviction (every $n$ steps instead of every step), and
(3)~fused CUDA kernels for block selection.
The overhead scales sub-linearly with context length (Figure~\ref{fig:scaling}):
62\% increase from 1800 to 6000 tokens ($3.3{\times}$ longer context).

\paragraph{Limitations.}
This study evaluates two 3B models on one benchmark family (RULER NIAH)
with actual context lengths up to ${\sim}$36K tokens (target 32\,000 tokens).
We deliberately chose NIAH as the primary benchmark because it provides a
\emph{controlled} evaluation that isolates eviction policy quality: the
task has a single, verifiable ground truth, the needle position and count
are parameterized, and scoring is deterministic
(\texttt{string\_match\_all}).
Open-ended generation benchmarks (e.g., LongBench summarization) conflate
eviction quality with generation variance, making it harder to attribute
accuracy differences to the eviction policy alone.
Our conclusions should therefore be interpreted primarily as evidence for
retrieval-oriented long-context settings rather than as a universal advantage
across all long-context generation tasks.
Nonetheless, extending to diverse benchmarks
(e.g., LongBench~\cite{bai2024longbench}, NoLiMa~\cite{modarressi2025nolima}),
longer contexts, larger models, and per-layer retention budgets are
important future directions.
The shared-index constraint (same tokens retained across all layers)
disadvantages methods designed for per-head selection and represents
another axis for improvement.

%% ================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ================================================================

We presented SieveKV, a query-aware KV cache eviction policy that
combines five lightweight signals with role-aware pinning and block retention.
On RULER NIAH, SieveKV achieves 70\% accuracy at $b{=}20\%$---surpassing
full KV (58\%) and SnapKV (30\%)---and reaches 80\% at the aggressive $b{=}10\%$
budget; the advantage grows with task complexity, reaching 92.5\% on 4-needle
retrieval vs.\ 80\% for full KV.
Cross-architecture transfer to Llama-3.2-3B confirms robustness (84\% accuracy,
within 6~points of full KV) without recalibration.
Ablation reveals strong signal synergy (single-component removal $\leq$4\%
degradation; removing all semantic signals collapses accuracy to 8\%).
Systems profiling shows per-step overhead of 8.86\,ms ($<$4\% of decode time),
scaling sub-linearly with context length, while eviction at $b{=}20\%$ reduces
the KV cache from 131\,MB to 26\,MB.

\paragraph{Future directions.}
Promising extensions include eviction--quantization co-design (e.g., combining
$b{=}20\%$ eviction with KIVI 2-bit quantization~\cite{liu2024kivi} for
${\sim}20{\times}$ memory reduction), learned adaptive signal weights for
improved cross-architecture robustness, per-layer retention budgets, and
evaluation on multi-hop reasoning and summarization workloads.
